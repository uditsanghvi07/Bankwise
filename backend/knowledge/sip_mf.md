# SIP and mutual funds (educational)

## KB:sip_mf-001

A **SIP (Systematic Investment Plan)** is just an automated monthly purchase of mutual fund units. It is a *behaviour* layered on top of any equity, debt, or hybrid mutual fund — not a separate product class. The two real benefits are **rupee-cost averaging** (you buy more units when NAV is low, fewer when it is high) and **discipline** (the e-mandate runs whether or not you feel like investing this month).

## KB:sip_mf-002

**Realistic long-run returns** (CAGR, nominal, post-expense, pre-tax) used in honest planning:

* Large-cap / Nifty 50 index funds: 10–12%.
* Multi-cap / flexi-cap funds: 11–13.5% with higher volatility.
* Hybrid / balanced advantage: 9–11%.
* PPF / EPF: 7–8.25% (admin-set, sovereign-backed, tax-favoured).
* Bank FDs: 6–7.25% (fully taxable at slab rate).
* Liquid funds: 6–7%.
* Gold ETFs: 7–9% nominal long-run, very high short-run noise.

These are educational ranges — the past 3-year window can show wildly different numbers. Plan with the lower end if you are not sure.

## KB:sip_mf-003

**SIP vs lump-sum**:

* Over a 7+ year horizon the difference between investing the same total amount as monthly SIP vs a single lump-sum is small (often within 1–2 percentage points of CAGR), and depends entirely on entry-point luck.
* SIP is overwhelmingly the right answer for **salaried** investors because it spreads timing risk and matches monthly income.
* Lump-sum makes sense only when you have a sudden inflow you cannot deploy in tranches (severance, bonus, inheritance) and the money is sitting idle in a savings account.

## KB:sip_mf-004

**Step-up SIP** — increasing the SIP amount automatically by 5–10% every year — is one of the highest-leverage habits in personal finance. On a 20-year horizon, a 10% annual step-up can add **30–40%** to the terminal corpus compared to a flat SIP, with almost no perceived monthly pinch as income grows.

## KB:sip_mf-005

**Common mutual-fund mistakes** in India:

* Chasing last year's top-quartile fund. Most "hot" funds revert to the median over the next 3–5 years.
* Holding 12+ funds across many AMCs. Above ~5 well-chosen funds, every additional one mostly duplicates exposure and adds tracking overhead.
* Using **NFO** launches as a shortcut. NFOs are marketing events; an existing fund with a 5+ year track record is usually a better choice.
* Stopping the SIP during a market crash. The crash is precisely when the rupee-cost-averaging math is doing its job.
* Forgetting **LTCG**: equity MF gains over ₹1.25 L/year are taxed at 12.5% (post-Jul-2024 rules), debt MF gains are taxed at slab rate with no indexation post-2023.

## KB:sip_mf-006

**A reasonable Indian middle-class default allocation** for a 7–15-year horizon (educational, not a recommendation):

* 50–60% diversified equity (large-cap index + flexi-cap).
* 25–35% debt (PPF + EPF + corporate-bond fund).
* 5–15% gold ETF / sovereign gold bond as inflation hedge.
* Rebalance once a year when any sleeve drifts more than 10 percentage points from target.
