# Home loans in India (educational)

## KB:home_loan-001

A **home loan** is a long-tenure secured loan (10–30 years) where the property is mortgaged to the lender as collateral. Indian retail home loans are now overwhelmingly **floating rate** and linked to an external benchmark (mostly **EBLR — Repo + spread**) since the RBI's October 2019 mandate. A small minority are still on the older MCLR or BPLR regimes; if you are on one, a switch to EBLR is usually free and immediate.

## KB:home_loan-002

**Loan-to-Value (LTV)** caps set by the RBI for housing loans (subject to occasional revision):

* Loan amount ≤ ₹30 L → LTV up to **90%** of property value.
* ₹30–75 L → LTV up to **80%**.
* > ₹75 L → LTV up to **75%**.

The remaining 10–25% is your down payment, plus stamp duty (5–8% of property value depending on the state) and registration (1–2%). Plan ~20–30% of the property value as out-of-pocket cash *over and above* the loan.

## KB:home_loan-003

**Components of a home loan EMI**:

* Principal repayment (rises every month).
* Interest on outstanding principal (falls every month).
* Optional: convenience / pre-EMI charges during construction.

The **amortisation curve** is heavily interest-loaded in the early years — for a 20-year loan, more than half of every EMI is interest in the first 5–6 years. This is why **prepayment in years 1–7 is dramatically more effective** at cutting total interest than prepayment near the end of the tenure.

## KB:home_loan-004

**Prepayment rules** (always read your sanction letter):

* Floating-rate retail home loans on individual borrowers — **no prepayment penalty** by RBI directive.
* Fixed-rate or partly-fixed loans — penalty often 1–2% of the prepaid amount.
* Some lenders cap "free prepayments" per year for very small partial payments; this is usually a fee on the *processing*, not a true penalty.

## KB:home_loan-005

**Balance transfer (BT)** is the legal switching of an existing home loan from Bank A to Bank B for a lower rate. It is worth doing when:

* The new rate is at least **30–50 bps** below your current rate.
* Remaining tenure is **5+ years** (so total interest savings exceed the BT fees: legal, technical valuation, and stamp duty on the new mortgage).
* Your CIBIL has improved meaningfully since the original sanction.

Always negotiate with your existing lender first by showing them the BT offer letter — many will match a chunk of the gap to retain you.

## KB:home_loan-006

**Tax benefits on home loans (old regime)**:

* Section 24(b) — interest deduction up to **₹2 L/yr** for self-occupied property.
* Section 80C — principal repayment up to **₹1.5 L/yr** (shared with PPF, ELSS, etc.).
* Section 80EEA — additional ₹1.5 L for first-time buyers on properties up to ₹45 L (eligibility window keeps moving by Budget; verify before claiming).

The **new tax regime** removes most of these benefits for self-occupied properties; rented properties still get interest deduction. Switch carefully — once chosen, the regime is locked for the year.

## KB:home_loan-007

**Common mistakes** Indian first-time buyers make:

* Stretching tenure to 30 years to fit FOIR — total interest can exceed the principal twice over.
* Choosing a builder-floated bank tie-up without comparing 3–4 lenders independently.
* Underestimating brokerage, society transfer charges, and registration when budgeting the down payment.
* Not registering an e-NACH mandate, and missing the first EMI due to paper-mandate delays.
* Forgetting that floating-rate EMIs *automatically* extend tenure when the rate goes up — most lenders default to "tenure adjustment", not "EMI adjustment".
