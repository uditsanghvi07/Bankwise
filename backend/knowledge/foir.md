# FOIR (Fixed Obligations to Income Ratio)

## KB:foir-001

FOIR measures what share of your **net monthly income** is already committed to EMIs (including the proposed loan). Indian retail lenders use FOIR caps as a primary filter before sanctioning home, personal, and auto loans. Net income excludes statutory deductions like income tax, EPF, and professional tax — most lenders compute FOIR on take-home pay.

## KB:foir-002

Typical **home loan** FOIR bands sit in the **40–55%** range depending on income slab and lender policy:

* Income ≤ ₹50K/month → most banks cap FOIR around 40%.
* ₹50K–₹1.2L/month → 45–50%.
* ₹1.2L–₹2.5L/month → 50–55%.
* ₹2.5L+/month → some lenders flex to 60–65% with strong CIBIL and stable employment.

Always confirm with the bank — these bands move with RBI prudential guidance and internal underwriting policy.

## KB:foir-003

FOIR is **not** the only underwriting factor. Credit score (CIBIL ≥ 750 commonly preferred), employment stability (typically 2+ years continuous), collateral quality / LTV (loan-to-value), age vs loan tenure, and policy exceptions all contribute. Two applicants with identical FOIR can get very different sanction letters.

## KB:foir-004

**Rule-of-thumb planning targets** that keep your household resilient:

* Total FOIR (existing + new) ≤ **40%** for medium-tenure goals.
* Home-loan EMI ≤ **30–35%** of net income on its own.
* Unsecured EMI (personal / credit-card / consumer-durable) ≤ **15%** combined.

Going above 50% FOIR sharply increases vulnerability to rate hikes, illness, or job loss. Anything above 60% is debt-trap territory in most household scenarios.

## KB:foir-005

**How banks compute the proposed EMI** in FOIR: they price the loan at their *current* card rate (or repo + spread) for the requested tenure, then add it to the sum of existing EMIs. If you negotiate a lower rate after sanction, your *real* FOIR drops below the underwriting figure — useful when arguing for a top-up later.
